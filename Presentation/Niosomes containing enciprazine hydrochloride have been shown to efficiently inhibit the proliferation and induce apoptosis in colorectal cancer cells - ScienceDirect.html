Niosomes containing enciprazine hydrochloride have been shown to efficiently inhibit the proliferation and induce apoptosis in colorectal cancer cells

Abstract

Colorectal cancer (CRC), currently the second most widespread cancer globally, exhibits a higher incidence in young individuals. Advancements have been made in developing anti-colorectal cancer drugs, including cytotoxic chemicals, in the past few decades. There is a need for new and innovative medications to overcome the current challenges in cancer treatment. Recent research examined the efficacy of innovative formulations in the prevention of colorectal cancer. In this study, we evaluated the efficacy of a niosome formulation loaded with Enciprazine hydrochloride (Nio-USAN). We assessed the anti-colorectal cancer characteristics of Nio-USAN by employing several techniques including CCK-8, invasion test, MTT test, flow cytometry, and cell cycle assessment. Quantitative real-time PCR was utilized to assess the transcription of genes linked to apoptosis. The F1-Nio-USAN and F2-Nio-USAN have average sizes of 200 and 500 nm, respectively. The entrapment effectiveness (EE%) of the F1-Nio-USAN and F2-Nio-USAN was measured to be 85.32 ± 0.27 % and 87.12 ± 0.35 %, respectively. The F1-Nio-USAN group exhibited the following percentages of HT-29 cell states: 43 % early apoptosis, 21 % late apoptosis, 7 % necrotic, and 29 % viable. The levels of transcription for cas8, Bid, BAX, cas9, and cas3 were significantly elevated in the treatment groups as compared to the PBS control group (P < 0.001). In addition, the treatment group exhibited significantly reduced levels of BCL2 gene transcription compared to the PBS control group (P < 0.01). These results suggest that it may be possible to improve the efficacy of USAN formulations in combating cancer by utilizing noisome encapsulation.

1. Introduction

Colorectal cancer (CRC) accounted for around 9.4 % of cancer-related fatalities in the United States, Europe, and Australia [1]. Colorectal malignancies have a prolonged development period, offering substantial potential for secondary prevention [2]. CRC is a leading global cause of mortality, responsible for around 16.5 million deaths in 2015. It is among the three most prevalent illnesses in males and is classified as the second most frequent carcinoma in women, behind breast cancer [3,4]. Colorectal cancer is often associated with older individuals; however, it is increasingly posing a risk to persons under the age of fifty [4]. Colorectal cancer is often seen in Western and Northern Europe as well as the USA, while it is seldom in Asian nations and India. It is projected that the incidence of colorectal cancer would rise by 60 % in the year 2030, resulting in a death rate of 1.1 million [5]. Metastasis, which is the spread of cancer, often indicates a stage four diagnosis of colorectal cancer (CRC) at its advanced stage. Several therapeutic approaches have been proposed; nevertheless, it is crucial to consider the potential adverse effects that may occur during and after therapy [[3], [4], [5]].
Multiple modalities have been employed in cancer management, such as immunotherapy, chemotherapy, surgical intervention, and radiation therapy [6]. Regrettably, the toxic nature of these therapies may have negative impacts on both healthy and malignant cells, resulting in side effects that include vomiting, gastrointestinal issues, reduced white blood cell count, and loss of hair [7]. Scientists are investigating new substances that might specifically target and destroy cancer cells while minimizing harm to healthy cells [7,8]. Cancer chemoprevention is the use of organic or synthetic substances derived from food or other substances to impede or decelerate the progression of cancer [9]. Chemoprevention is becoming more popular because it may effectively decrease cancer-related diseases while minimizing severe adverse effects [10]. Piperazines and similar pharmaceuticals efficiently preserve several bioactive molecules that have the potential to function as potent anticancer agents [11].
Enciprazine hydrochloride (USAN) is a pharmacological compound that functions as an anxiolytic agent, belonging to the class of non-benzodiazepine drugs [12]. It has less sedative/hypnotic adverse reactions, and its association with alcohol is much lower compared to benzodiazepines [13]. Enciprazine displays a range of biological effects, including cardiovascular, hypotensive, and local anaesthetic characteristics [12,13]. Enciprazine is a medication that belongs to the phenylpiperazine family. It is used to treat anxiety and psychosis [12,13]. However, it was never made available for marketing. It is strongly attracted to the α1-adrenergic receptor and 5-HT1A receptor, as well as other locations [13,14]. The medicine was initially expected to generate ortho-methoxyphenylpiperazine (oMeOPP), a potent serotonin receptor agonist with a strong affinity for the 5-HT1A receptor, as a notable active metabolite. However, further investigation revealed that this was not the actual outcome. Enciprazine, often referred to as D-3112 and WY-48624, is a substance that activates GABA receptors and has the potential to be used for the management of anxiety conditions [14,15].
Enciprazine is an analogue that has shown encouraging outcomes as an anticancer medication [12,16]. This compound is a kinase blocker that can impede the proliferation of cancerous cells in both human and Chinese hamster ovarian cell types [17]. Enciprazine also has apoptotic impacts on tumor cells, triggering regulated cell death. Moreover, Enciprazine has shown the ability to suppress hepcidin, a hormone responsible for controlling iron metabolism and implicated in the advancement of cancer. This medication exhibits promise as an innovative anticancer treatment owing to its capacity to selectively affect several pathways implicated in the genesis and advancement of cancer. Enciprazine has also been shown to act as a suppressor of ranitidine absorption into urine, potentially affecting the management of stomach ulcers [12,16,17].
Utilizing nanosystems for delivering medicinal substances to the targeted site of action is a captivating and valuable area of research in medication investigation [18]. Novel drug delivery methods have significantly enhanced the therapeutic effectiveness and safety of conventional medications, particularly for compounds in nanoscale [19]. In addition, they have the potential to provide regulated or sustained release routes to mitigate the advancement of negative consequences [18,19]. Optimally, efficient delivery mechanisms are required to protect oral medications from the corrosive effects of stomach acid and digestive enzymes. These strategies mitigate the negative impacts of food consumption on the absorption of medicine while enhancing the accessibility of the drug in the body [20]. Reports indicate that niosomes tend to selectively enter the membranes of tumor cells and demonstrate anticancer effects via many pathways, including cellular enzymatic activity, inhibition of blood vessel formation, immune system stimulation, protection against oxidative damage, and induction of cell death (apoptosis). Furthermore, niosomes are a benign and biocompatible natural material [20,21].
In addition, niosomes possess more durability compared to liposomes and serve as effective delivery methods and non-toxic nanoparticles for therapeutic applications [[19], [20], [21]]. Niosomes consist of non-ionic surfactants and can improve the entry of substances into the cytoplasm. The distribution techniques for noisome nanoparticles involve intravenous, intramuscular, intrathecal, and epidermal approaches. Several drug niosomal compositions have been studied using targeted, controlled, and sustained release techniques [[19], [20], [21]]. Enciprazine niosomal compositions have the potential to enhance their ability to cross the blood-brain barrier, optimize their pharmacokinetic properties when taken orally, and serve as a topical treatment for neurological symptoms. Enciprazine has been proven in many studies for its anticancer characteristics, establishing it as a promising research topic. This work introduces a distinct formulation of Enciprazine that is encased in niosomes. The primary aim of our research is to thoroughly examine the medicinal chemistry, drug transport method, and possible therapeutic applications of Enciprazine, particularly its impact on cancer cells.

2. Experimental

2.1. Materials

Enciprazine hydrochloride (USAN), cholesterol, propylene glycol (PG), polyethylene glycol 200 (PEG 200), polyethylene glycol 400 (PEG 400), and ethanol were acquired from Merck Co. in Germany. Span 20, manufactured by Samchun Pure Chemical Co., Ltd. in Korea, was employed in addition to tween 20 and dichloromethane, supplied by Sharlua and Sharlab S.L. in Spain, respectively. The substances such as agarose, ethidium bromide, ethylene diamine tetra acetic acid (EDTA), polyvinyl alcohol, MTT package, annexin V FITC, propidium iodide, and Trypan blue were acquired from Sigma-Aldrich, USA. All the other compounds utilized during the investigation were procured from local sources and met the standards of analytical grade.

2.2. Niosome encapsulated enciprazine hydrochloride (Nio- USAN) preparation

The nanosized particles vesicles were prepared using the thin film hydration technique [30]. In order to create niosomes containing USAN, a certain amount of cholesterol, nonionic surfactants, and USAN were completely dissolved in 25 mL of dichloromethane. This was done in a dry and clean round bottom flask. The transparent solution was transformed into a thin, desiccated layer using a rotating vacuum evaporator (Heidolph; Germany) at a temperature of 60 °C. Subsequently, a volume of 25 mL of highly purified water was utilized to rehydrate a thin desiccated film. The temperature was then elevated to 100 °C and the mixture was allowed to remain for a duration of 30 min to facilitate hydration and the creation of niosomes (Table 1). Ultimately, the mixture was immersed in a container filled with ice and exposed to a process called sonication, which was repeated five times. Each cycle lasted 2 min and had an amplitude of 20 %. The sonication was performed using a probe sonicator manufactured by Bandelin in Germany.

2.3. Characterization of Nio- USAN

The diameter and poly-dispersity index (PDI) of niosomes were determined using dynamic light scattering (DLS) using a Zetasizer Nano ZS system (Malvern Instruments Worcestershire: UK) at an angle of 90° and a temperature of 25 °C. The zeta potential of noisomes was measured using Laser Doppler electrophoresis.

2.4. Encapsulation efficiency (EE %)

In order to calculate the EE% (encapsulation efficiency percentage), the USAN (USAN loaded with niosomes) was subjected to centrifugation at a speed of 15,000 revolutions per minute for a duration of 30 min. This was followed by filtering of the resulting liquid through a filter with a pore size of 0.22 μm. Subsequently, the concentration of USAN in the solution that has been filtered (free drug) was quantified using High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) at a wavelength of 430 nm. The HPLC system used was Kneuer from Germany, fitted with a Kneuer XDB-C18 column (5 μm, 4.6 × 250 mm). The mobile phase, composed of a mixture of acetonitrile, water, and methanol at a ratio of 35:45:20, was given at a flow rate of 1 ml/min. According to the HPLC findings, the USAN had a retention duration of 4.9 min. The EE % was determined utilizing equation (1).EE% = (Winitial- Wfree)/Winitial
Winitial denotes the quantity of the medication that was introduced to the formulation, whereas Wfree reflects the quantity of the drug remaining in the supernatant.

2.5. Morphology analysis

The morphology of niosomes was analyzed using scanning electron microscopy. The chemical composition underwent a process of gold coating using the nano-structure coating DSR1. The coated composition was then examined using a scanning electron microscope (HITACHI S-4160) at a magnification of 15,000 and an acceleration voltage of 20.0 kV.

2.6. Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) analysis

The USAN-excipent interaction was evaluated using a Cary 630 F T-IR spectro-photometer (Agilent Technologies Inc., CA; USA) equipped with a diamond ATR (attenuated total reflectance). Cholesterol, USAN, tween 20, and span 20 underwent FT-IR analysis. The spectra were obtained by applying a tiny quantity of each uniform sample onto the window and scanning throughout the wave number range of 400–4000 cm-1 with a resolution of 2 cm-1.

2.7. Evaluation of Nio- USAN release and stability

Dialysis was used to investigate the emission of the noxious substance by USAN. The dialysis device was immersed in distilled water for a duration of 24 h. A total of 0.5 ml (10 mg) of Nio-USAN was placed inside the dialysis bag. Additionally, a control sample consisting of 0.5 ml of USAN liquid solution comprising 10 mg of USAN was employed. The dialysis bags were submerged in conical flasks containing 75 ml of distilled water and agitated at a speed of 50 rpm in a water bath maintained at a temperature of 37 °C. A volume of 5 ml was extracted from the receptor media at certain time intervals of 1, 2, 4, 6, 12, and 24 h. The concentration of USAN was determined using spectrophotometry at a wavelength of 281 nm. Subsamples were substituted with a fresh medium at a temperature of 37 °C, and the diffusion pattern was assessed utilizing kinetic models. The stability of several Nio-USAN formulations' diffusion was monitored using this approach. Measurements were taken at intervals of 0, 15, 30, 45 and 60 days throughout a two-month storage period at a temperature of 4 and 25 °C.

2.8. Cell culture

The HT-29 Colorectal cell lines were obtained from the Iranian Biological Resource Centre (IBRC) and then cultured following the guidelines supplied by ATCC. Colorectal cell lines were cultivated in Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium (DMEM; Gibco) at a temperature of 37 °C and a carbon dioxide concentration of 5 %. The medium was enriched with 10 % foetal bovine serum (FBS), 50 μg/ml of penicillin, and 50 μg/ml of streptomycin (Sigma-Aldrich, USA).

2.9. Examine the expansion and multiplication of CCK-8 colonies

The colon cancer cells in both the experimental group, which received nanoparticle therapy, and the control group, which did not receive nanoparticle treatment, were analyzed for CCK-8 utilizing a CCK-8 detection kit (Dojindo, Japan). After being implanted into growth plates, the cells were subjected to nanoparticle therapy. The cell lines were cultured for a duration of 10 h prior to the administration of the CCK-8 chemical treatment. The absorbance has been measured at a wavelength of 450 nm.

2.10. Transwell incursion detections

The target and control groups of colorectal cancer cells were not populating the 24-well transwell container (BD Biosciences, USA) until the adherent cells had already established a layer on its surface. The areas that were invaded were subjected to a treatment with a fixative called 4 % paraformaldehyde, and then labelled with Giemsa dye after a period of 24 h. Afterwards, the upper parts were meticulously cleaned. Afterwards, the cells were analyzed utilizing an optical microscope.

2.11. Wound-healing assay

HT-29 cells were introduced at a density of 106 cells per well onto 6-well culture plates that were pre-coated with fibronectin at a dosage of 240 μg/ml. The cell monolayers were punctured utilizing a pipette tip and then rinsed with PBS after a 24-h incubation period. The culture media was replaced with DMEM supplemented with 1 % fetal calf serum. After being incubated for 18 h, the cells from the F1-Nio-USAN, F2-Nio-USAN, and Free USAN (240 μg/ml) groups were observed. The observation included counting the number of cells that migrated towards the injured areas.

2.12. Cancer cell proliferation assay

The cell viability of HT-29 colon cancer cells underwent treatment with F1-Nio-USAN, F2-Nio-USAN, and Free USAN was validated using the MTT cell viability Kit I (Roche, Switzerland) colorimetric assay. The 96-well flat-bottomed plate was inoculated with 5 × 105 cells per well, and the cells were cultured at a temperature of 37 °C with a carbon dioxide concentration of 5 %. The cells were assessed for vitality after being washed twice with PBS over the course of a day in each well. Each well was treated with 100 μl of serum-free medium containing 5 μg/ml of Sigma MTT. The treated wells were then put in a CO2 incubator and kept at a temperature of 37 °C for a duration of 4 h. The medium was progressively removed, and DMSO was introduced. The optical density at 570 nm was measured using a State Fax-2100 ELISA plate scanner (Awareness Palm City, FL) to determine the level of MTT metabolism that produces blue formazan, while taking into account the background.

2.13. Detection of apoptosis using flow cytometry

Five hundred and fifty-five cells were injected in six-well plates for every well containing HT-29 cells. Following a 12-h incubation period, the cells adhered to the plates. Following a 24-h incubation period, the media was removed and replaced with 2 ml of DMEM solution for each well. The Annexin V-PI cell apoptosis kit, supplied by Biyuntian Biotech Co. Ltd., was used to assess apoptosis in three groups: F1-Nio-USAN, F2-Nio-USAN, and Free USAN. Apoptosis was identified using a Becton–Dickinson FACScan for flow cytometry.

2.14. Cell cycle analysis

The cells were immersed in 100 % ethanol for a duration of 24 h in order to maintain their integrity. After undergoing two rounds of washing with PBS, the cells were stained for 15 min employing the PI/RNase labelling solution obtained from BD Bioscience Pharmingen. The DNA makeup of the cell population was analyzed employing FACS flow cytometry. The cell cycle information was analyzed using FlowJo V10 software, developed by Tree Star in Ashland, Oregon.

2.15. Transcription of apoptosis-related genes by quantitative real-time PCR

The transcription amounts of the cas8, Bid, BAX, cas9, and cas3, and BCL2 genes were quantified employing a quantitative real-time PCR method with SYBR green detection. The Total RNA was obtained using the RNX TM-PLUS technique (sinaclon, Iran) as instructed by the manufacturer. Subsequently, the Parsian BioProducts Manufacturers Kit (PBP, Iran) produced cDNA. We conducted a quantitative real-time PCR analysis utilizing the SYBR® Premix Ex TaqTM II kit from (Takara, Japan) along with the primers specified in Table 2. We followed the guidelines provided by the manufacturer. We standardize the transcript levels of each gene by comparing it to the GAPDH reference gene, which enables us to determine the relative amounts of gene transcription. Two separate time intervals were utilized for conducting the research.

2.16. Statistical analysis

The information was evaluated and statistical analysis was performed using GraphPad Prism 5.0. Only the mean and standard deviation were shown as data points. A one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was employed to compare the means, followed by a Tukey-Kramer post hoc analysis with a 95 % confidence interval. The differences demonstrated statistical significance; they were considered significant at a P-value of less than 0.05 and highly significant at a P-value of less than 0.001.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Characteristics of synthesized niosomes

Nio-USAN was synthesized using the film hydration process. This approach utilized different proportions of nonionic surfactants (a combination of tween 20 and span 20) to cholesterol. Table 3 displays the components of Nio-USAN and their corresponding characteristics. Table 3 indicated a significant reduction in the particle size of the F1 formulation, from 500 nm to 200 nm (P < 0.01). According to reports, the existence of cholesterol increased the cohesiveness of the bi-layer and caused a decrease in the size of niosomes due to increased rigidity.

Table 3 further demonstrated that the F1 formulation resulted in a decrease in the zeta potential. In the F2 formulation, the zeta potential ratio was 0.75 ± 0.08. A desirable characteristic for F1 formulation is a maximum zeta potential of 0.42 ± 0.05 mV. This characteristic enhances the durability of niosomes during storage and when combined with water, contributing to the electrostatic stabilization of the formulation. Furthermore, it was observed that the zeta value of particles exhibited an increase as the proportion of USAN entrapment dropped. This phenomenon may be attributed to the dispersion of the unentrapped USAN either in the water phase or in the diffusion layer.
The PDI-value often fell within the range of 0–1, with numbers close to 0 indicating a uniform dispersion. The PDI (polydispersity index) value, as shown in Table 3, ranged from 0.184 ± 0.007 to 0.397 ± 0.004, with a significance level of P < 0.01. Typically, PDI values above 0.7 indicate a significant variation in the size of particles. Table 3 clearly demonstrates that both F1 and F2 formulations exhibited a PDI value smaller than 0.7.

3.2. FeSEM analysis

The FeSEM images revealed that the niosomes had a spherical shape and were clearly separated from one another (Fig. 1A and B). The photographs also revealed that the niosomes had a consistent size, which is a distinctive feature of their morphological characteristics.

3.3. FTIR

FTIR spectroscopy was employed to investigate the potential interaction between the USAN and the niosomal particles at a molecular scale (Fig. 1C and D). Fig. 1 presents a summary of the data obtained from the FTIR spectra of F1-Nio-USAN, and F2-Nio-USAN. The FTIR spectra of F1-Nio-USAN exhibited a characteristic broad peak at 3563.48 cm-1, which corresponds to the stretching of O-H bonds. Additionally, there were peaks seen at 1642.26 cm-1, indicating stretching of C-C and C-O bonds, and at 1254.32 cm-1, indicating stretching of C-O bonds. The peaks of F2-Nio-USAN were seen at specific wavenumbers: 2630.58 cm-1 for O–H stretching, 1563.48 cm-1 for C–H stretching, 856.88 cm-1 for C–H bending, and 1054 cm-1 for C–O stretching. The FTIR spectra clearly exhibit the typical peaks for USAN, as depicted in Fig. 1. The FTIR analysis confirmed that there were no chemical reactions between USAN and the excipient.

3.4. In-vitro drug release and stability analysis

The rapid release of USAN resulted in a total diffusion of 53.5 ± 1.4 and 67.4 ± 0.71, in F1-Nio-USAN, F2-Nio-USAN, respectively, after 120 h (Fig. 2A). Avoiding an intensive release is recommended to maintain the drug's efficacy and prevent unwanted effects on the human body. The rapidity of the initial discharge can be ascribed to the medications present on the surface. Over time, additional enclosed medicines will be discharged from the surface. After 9 h, the concentration of USAN on the surface will be minimized, allowing the enclosed pharmaceuticals in the inner layers to begin releasing. As a result, the rate of drug release will decrease.
The release of USAN from niosome nanoparticles exhibited a biphasic pattern consisting of an initial burst phase followed by a stable phase. The stable phase reached its maximum level after 9 h in two different formulations. The quantity of USAN that was released initially decreased, whereas the rate of release gradually rose over a period of 72 h. Higher effectiveness of encapsulation and more successful controlled drug release were seen in cases where lower speeds were used in the blast phase of drug release. Consequently, formulations F1 and F2 exhibited the highest levels of controlled emission efficiency, with release proportions of 21 ± 0.9 and 31 ± 2.3, respectively, during the blast phase. The F1 formulation exhibited the greatest efficacy in controlled diffusion, with a drug release of 53.5 ± 1.4 % after 120 h. In comparison, the release of the free USAN drug was 81 % during the initial blast phase, which then climbed to 99 % after 72 h (Fig. 2A).
The stability of F1-Nio-USAN and F2-Nio-USAN was evaluated by examining their morphologic properties, including average dimensions and encapsulation efficiency (EE), under different temperatures and time durations. The findings indicated that elevated temperatures had an impact on the morphological characteristics of F1-Nio-USAN and F2-Nio-USAN, leading to an enlargement in size and a reduction in drug EE. However, prolonging the lifespan did not have a significant effect on either the drug EE or the morphological characteristics (Fig. 2B). The results indicate that different drug formulations exhibited greater stability at 4 °C compared to 25 °C, suggesting that temperature has a detrimental effect on the stability of the encapsulated drugs. Conversely, the F1 formulation of USAN-encapsulated niosome exhibited the highest level of stability compared to the other formulations.

3.5. Wound healing analysis

The migratory process is an essential characteristic of cancer cells that actively contributes to the spread of the disease. The study utilized HT-29 cells in in vitro wound (scratch) experiments to assess the effects of medications on migration, invasion, and the rate of complete wound healing in the scratched region. The HT-29 cell lines treated with F1-Nio-USAN and F2-Nio-USAN showed decreased cell migration in comparison to the free USAN. Fig. 3A shows that HT-29 cells treated with F1-Nio-USAN did not display a statistically significant decrease in scratch width (86 μm) after 18 h, in comparison to the scratch width of cells treated with F2-Nio-USAN (82 μm). The size of the scratches on HT-29 cells, which were treated with free USAN, was measured to be 53 μm after 18 h.

3.6. The Nio-USAN inhibits the growth and invasion of cancerous cells in vitro

The results of the colony formation experiment and CCK-8 test demonstrated that the USAN medication effectively inhibited the proliferation of HT-29 cells (Fig. 3B). In addition, the F1-Nio-USAN and F2-Nio-USAN treatments significantly decreased the growth of HT-29 cells (P < 0.01 and P < 0.05, respectively). The transwell invasion study revealed that the use of F1-Nio-USAN and F2-Nio-USAN to HT-29 cells resulted in a reduction of invasive cancer cells by 35 % and 61 % respectively. Conversely, there was no noticeable change in the PBS groups. Consequently, the growth and spread of these malignant cells were inhibited in a laboratory setting with the administration of F1-Nio-USAN and F2-Nio-USAN.

3.7. Nio-USAN shown a reduction in the viability of cancer cells

MTT evaluated the impact of several formulations on the vitality of colorectal cancer cells after 24 h. After 24 h, there was a significant difference in the growth rates of the control, F1-Nio-USAN, and F2-Nio-USAN groups (P < 0.001). Additionally, the F1-Nio-USAN group exhibited a lower growth proportion of colorectal cancer cells. The rate of cell growth in the colorectal cancer cells of the Free USAN groups did not show a significant difference (P > 0.05) (Fig. 4A). This finding demonstrates that the Nio-USAN has a substantial adverse effect on the proliferation of colorectal cancer cells.

3.8. Nio-USAN increased apoptosis rate using flow cytometry

Based on the data presented in Fig. 4B, the cancer cells in the group that was treated exhibited significantly higher rates of apoptosis compared with the cells in the control group. In addition, the group receiving therapy for cancer observed a decrease in the vitality of cancer cells. Nevertheless, in the control groups treated with PBS, over 90 % of the cells remained viable, in contrast to less than 10 % of cells in the experimental populations that were in early, late, or necrotic states. Early apoptosis, late apoptosis, necrotic, and alive HT-29 cells were seen in 43 %, 21 %, 7 %, and 29 % of the cells with F1-Nio-USAN, respectively. The proportion of F2-Nio-USAN treated HT-29 cells that had early apoptosis, late apoptosis, necrosis, and remained alive was 29 %, 25 %, 10 %, and 36 %, respectively. The percentages of early apoptosis, late apoptosis, necrosis, and live cells in the HT-29 cells treated with Free USAN were 12 %, 14 %, 5 %, and 69 % correspondingly.
Cell cycle analysis was conducted using flow cytometry. Sub-G1 levels were shown to be higher in the target cell lines (F1-Nio-USAN, F2-Nio-USAN, Free USAN) compared to the PBS groups, resulting in a delay in the progression of cells into the G2 phase. Therefore, among this group, there was a reduction in cellular proliferation. Based on the cell cycle studies, F1-Nio-USAN primarily arrested the cell cycle in the G0/G1 phase (Fig. 4C and D).

3.9. Nio-USAN enhanced apoptosis rate by activation of Cas8 genes

The transcription in HT-29 cells lines of the proapoptotic genes cas8, Bid, BAX, cas9, and cas3, as well as the antiapoptotic BCL2 genes, was analyzed using real-time PCR. The quantity of proapoptotic transcription were considerably higher in the Free-USAN, F2-Nio-USAN, and F1-Nio-USAN treatment groups compared to the PBS groups (P < 0.05, P < 0.01, and P < 0.001, respectively) (Fig. 5). Subsequently, we analyzed the transcription of the BCL2 antiapoptotic gene in HT-29 cells. The transcription of the BCL2 gene was significantly higher in the PBS control cell line compared to the Free-USAN (P < 0.05), F2-Nio-USAN (P < 0.01), and F1-Nio-USAN (P < 0.001) treated cells, respectively. The general diagram illustrating the process of apoptosis initiation by Nio-USAN with caspase 8 activation is depicted in Fig. 6.
Despite tremendous advancements in improving the survival rates of early-stage cancer patients, the present treatment approaches are still unsatisfactory, primarily because of the absence of viable therapeutic methods [22]. In our study, we searched for novel therapeutic agents by examining the substance Enciprazine hydrochloride. We assessed its effectiveness in treating colorectal cancer, which is one of the most commonly diagnosed forms of cancer. Additionally, we investigated the specific processes by which Enciprazine hydrochloride acts against this type of cancer.
This study aimed to examine the influence of Nio-USAN on the encapsulating and release properties of the USAN medicine. The study examined the drug release process using dialysis bags containing different formulations. Compared to previous reports, our attained EE percentage is significantly higher [23]. A researcher in this area disclosed the production of nanoparticles carrying drugs with an encapsulation efficiency (EE%) that varied from 66.4 % to 67.5 % [24]. The release of USAN in this investigation occurred in two phases. The initial release phase lasted for 9 h, followed by a subsequent period of slower release. We have developed a novel method to improve USAN's encapsulation efficiency (EE) in niosome and obtain smaller particles and lower PDI scores. The main differentiating factor of this method, as opposed to others, is in the initial volume of the water-based solution and the ratio of the organic phase to the water phase. The results are consistent with previous studies, which suggest that passive loading often results in an encapsulation efficiency (EE) of approximately 1 % for most medicines [25]. The inefficiency of this process leads to substantial medication wastage. Drugs such as USAN often have a low encapsulation efficiency (EE) due to their tendency to rush, leading to the drug being lost in the aqueous phase [26]. The limited effectiveness of USAN encapsulation is due to the more significant presence of the aqueous phase in the exterior environment of the niosomes, in contrast to the limited aqueous capacity within the niosomes themselves [[26], [27], [28]]. Applying pressure during this procedure may lead to a higher release of this USAN, which can cause a drop in EE [28,29].
A prolonged release should be avoided since it may lessen the medication's effectiveness and negatively affect the body [30,31]. The medications' initial rapid release can be primarily due to them. Over time, more medicine capsules are discharged from the surface [32]. Previous research revealed that diffusion and biodegradability were the main variables influencing drug release from polymeric nanoparticles. The explosive release was characterized by a rapid drug release within the initial few hours [33]. Both this experiment and previous studies show that the entrapped drug was explosively released [34,35]. The interaction of charges within the USAN and the niosome, which occurs during the continuous release of the medicine, is crucial [36].
Extending the storage period does not impact the structure or effectiveness of the drug's encapsulation. A study was carried out to evaluate the stability of the synthetic drug utilizing nanotechnology. The results indicated that temperature has a substantial impact on the breakdown of the medication [37]. The nanoparticles underwent morphological changes akin to a gradual temperature increase over 60 days, decreasing their efficacy. Nevertheless, the findings of this study suggest that nanostructure drugs can be stored for 15–30 days without experiencing a substantial decline in efficacy. Existing research suggests that temperature serves as a limiting factor in the field of pharmacology [38]. As temperatures climb, the amount of lipid increases, resulting in a rise in the release of medicines. As a result, the lipid complex termed niosome is unstable when exposed to ambient temperature. Applying Tween 20 to the niosome ensures its stability at room temperature [39].
Enciprazine hydrochloride is a pharmaceutical compound that has received limited research attention about its potential as an anticancer agent [40]. Furthermore, niosome formulation has resulted in a more significant cytotoxic impact on cancer cells. This allows for a reduced medicine dosage, leading to fewer adverse effects on healthy organs and tissues [41]. The association between the dosage of USAN and its toxicity has been found in studies where cancer cells were exposed to either free USAN or a niosome containing USAN. Interestingly, the data showed that the toxicity of free USAN was lower than that of USAN-loaded niosomes when given at the exact dosage. These results would be logical if the drug's antiproliferative effect were enhanced by niosomal encapsulation.
As anticipated, the niosomes lacking pharmaceuticals exhibited minimal cytotoxicity on the treated cells. This finding confirms that niosomes are a safe option for delivering drugs to humans [42]. The compounds USAN and Nio-USAN exhibited cytotoxic effects on HT-29 cells by causing apoptosis, as evidenced by flow cytometric examination. The procedure of producing the niosomes did not modify the drug's mode of action. The results support prior studies demonstrating the importance of the balance between pro- and anti-apoptotic proteins, specifically those produced by the cas8, Bid, BAX, cas9, and cas3 genes, in the growth and proliferation of cancer cells [43]. The mRNA levels of cas8, Bid, BAX, cas9, and cas3 were dramatically increased. Upregulated expression of apoptosis-related proteins, such as caspase-8, Bid, BAX, caspase-9, and caspase-3. The phenomenon of nanoparticles' capacity to specifically accumulate and infiltrate cells is a captivating subject that warrants additional investigation. Hybrid lipid polymer nanoparticles enhance the effectiveness of anticancer drugs by infiltrating cancer cells, dispersing within the cytoplasm, and evading degradation within endosomes [44].
Prior research on drug delivery has mostly concentrated on developing different nanocarriers, such as liposomes, polymeric nanoparticles, and solid lipid nanoparticles [45]. The main objectives of these studies were to boost the solubility and stability of drugs and improve their pharmacokinetics and effectiveness. Several studies have indicated an enhancement in the anticancer activity of the medicine when tested in laboratory conditions [46,47]. However, there is still a requirement for a delivery system that can optimize the absorption of the drug and regulate its release within the body.

4. Conclusions

The recent experiment showed that niosomal USAN is an efficient, secure, and adequate delivery strategy. The synthesized Nio-USAN exhibited a consistent drug release profile and had homogeneous nanoscale dimensions in physiological conditions. The encapsulation of USAN resulted in increased physicochemical properties and drug stability, leading to enhanced bioactivity of the medication. The Nio-USAN formulations demonstrated the ability to induce apoptosis in HT-29 cancer cell lines in a laboratory setting, potentially through the modulation of apoptosis-related genes. The effective delivery of chemotherapeutic medications using nanotechnology depends on the location of niosomes within cells and their relationship with living cells. The therapeutic advantages of niosomal formulations can be improved through their intracellular localization and extended persistence within cancer cells. The outcomes of this work indicate a potentially effective method for creating a safe and effective customized medication delivery method for the in-vitro prevention of colon cancer.
