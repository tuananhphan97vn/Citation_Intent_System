Mutual Influence of Contact Processes in the Simultaneous Interaction of Active and Inert Metal Melts with ZrO 2 Ceramics

The processes occurring in the simultaneous contact of ZrO 2 ceramics with two metal melts, inert (Cu, Cu–Ga, Ge, Cu–Ge) and active (Cu–Ga–Ti, Cu–Ti), were studied. The experiments were conducted in a high vacuum using thin ZrO 2 ceramic plates, with one side in contact with a droplet of active melt and the opposite side with a droplet of inert melt. In the simultaneous interaction of active and inert metal melts with zirconium dioxide, the interface processes showed mutual influence: oxygen-deficient zirconium dioxide (ZrO 2– x ) was formed through the absorption of oxygen from ZrO 2 by the active melt. This contributed to the dissolution of zirconium from the solid oxide in the inert melt, thereby activating it and increasing its adhesion to the substrate. At the same time, the dissolution of zirconium in the inert melt reduced the oxygen deficiency in zirconium dioxide, i.e., restoring its stoichiometry and promoting further absorption of oxygen by the active melt. Thus, with the simultaneous contact of active and inert melts with ZrO 2 , a larger amount of oxygen dissolves in the active melt, which leads to the saturation of the active melt with oxygen. This results in effects such as the loss of metallic luster and spherical shape of droplets in the active melt, delamination of the active melt, and increase in the thickness of the transition layers at the interface between the active melt and ZrO 2 . The results are significantly influenced by the amounts of inert and active melts in contact with ZrO 2 and by the concentration of the active component (titanium) in the system. The results can be used in the development of methods for brazing and metallization of ceramic materials and techniques for the manufacture and use of refractories and high-temperature electrochemical devices.

Introduction

The addition of an active component with a high affinity for oxygen (particularly titanium) to metal melts improves their wetting of solid oxides through surface interaction in the system [ 1 ]. The paper [ 2 ] studied the simultaneous contact of active (Cu–Ti, Ni–Ti) and inert (Cu, Ni, Ge, Cu–Ga, Cu–Ge, etc.) metal melts with zirconium dioxide to assess how highly nonstoichiometric zirconium dioxide influences their wetting by inert metal melts. Significant improvement in ZrO 2 wetting was observed as zirconium from the nonstoichiometric substrate (ZrO 2– x → ZrO 2 + Zr) dissolved in the inert metal. To ensure maximum oxygen deficiency, i.e., a constant outflow of oxygen from the solid oxide, a significant amount of active metal melts was used in the experiments. The experiments followed a ‘sandwich’ pattern, where the active melt was formed from a relatively low-melting eutectic between titanium and copper or nickel. The active melt contacted with the lower side of a ZrO 2 ceramic plate, while an inert melt droplet was placed on its upper side. In this way, the deoxidation effect was achieved.

In the simultaneous contact of inert and active metals with ZrO 2 , the interfacial processes are mutually influential. This is particularly due to the continuous dissolution of zirconium in the inert melt, which restores the substrate’s stoichiometry and promotes further oxygen dissolution in the active melt. This interaction influences both the wetting and interfacial processes. These effects are likely to be especially pronounced when only small amounts of the active component (titanium) are present in the system, as it may not achieve complete deoxidation.

The objective is to study the mutual influence of simultaneous contact of inert and active metal melts with ZrO 2 when the amount of active melt in the samples is much lower than in those analyzed in [ 2 ].

Experimental Procedure

Wetting was studied using the sessile drop method. The experiments were conducted in a vacuum of at least 10 –3 Pa at temperatures ranging from 1323 to 1423 K. Images of the droplets were captured with a high-resolution digital camera.

The experiments were conducted with dense ZrO 2 ceramics partially stabilized with 3.5 at.% Y 2 O 3 , high-purity (not lower than 99.997%) Cu, Ga, and Ge metals, and iodide-refined titanium. The inert metal melts were copper, germanium, and Cu–17.5 Ga and Cu–20 Ge alloys, with peritectic compositions as specified in [ 3 , 4 ] and liquidus temperatures of 1193 and 973 K, respectively. To prepare the Cu–Ga and Cu–Ge alloys, their weighed portions were melted in a vacuum in graphite crucibles, and part of the Cu–Ga ingots was rolled to a thickness of 0.1 mm.

The copper–titanium and copper–gallium–titanium systems, in particular the Cu–17.5 Ga alloy with various titanium additions, were chosen as active metal melts. The active alloys were prepared by preliminary melting in a vacuum on a calcium fluoride substrate [ 5 ] or directly during the wetting experiments: weighed portions of the Cu–17.5 Ga alloy and titanium were placed on a ZrO 2 ceramic sample, allowing titanium to dissolve in the Cu–Ga alloy after melting. This resulted in the required melt composition.

The ceramic samples were cut into 1–2 mm thick plates and polished on both sides with 0.7–0.3 μm diamond paste. Alumina ceramics, graphite, molybdenum wire, and 0.2 mm thick molybdenum plates were used as auxiliary materials. U-shaped supports were made of Al 2 O 3 ceramics and graphite to hold the ceramic plates (Fig. 1 ). A setup was also employed where Al 2 O 3 ceramic spacers were placed on an Al 2 O 3 ceramic substrate, with the ceramic plate under study positioned on top.

The experiments in which the active and inert melts simultaneously interacted with ZrO 2 involved several setups:

  • Preliminary formation of contacts between the active alloy and ZrO 2 ceramic sample. A droplet of the active alloy was melted on a ceramic plate in a vacuum to form an adhesive bond between the melt and the oxide. After cooling, the plate was placed on a U-shaped support with the solidified droplet facing downward. A weighed portion of inert metal was then placed on the upper side of the plate, and the assembly was heated in a vacuum while both droplets were observed.

  • Formation of contacts between the inert and active metals and ZrO 2 in the experiment. A weighed, flat portion of inert metal was placed beneath the U-shaped substrate. A ZrO 2 ceramic plate was then positioned on the substrate, with weighed portions of active metal placed on top. The assembly was heated in a vacuum. As the inert metal melted, the droplet became spherical, rising to touch the lower side of the ceramic plate, thereby forming a contact between the metal and the oxide.

  • Use of a molybdenum plate. A Cu–17.5 Ga alloy foil was placed on a molybdenum plate with a ZrO 2 ceramic plate positioned on top. A weighted portion of active metal was then placed on the ceramic plate. The assembly was heated in a vacuum. The Cu–17.5 Ga alloy wets molybdenum sufficiently well, preventing the melt from flowing out of the gap between the molybdenum and ceramic plates and thereby ensuring contact between the inert metal and the solid oxide.

Results and Discussion

Figure 2 shows photographs of a sample in which a ZrO 2 ceramic plate separates (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti (top) and pure copper (bottom) droplets. Immediately after copper melts, the active melt droplet loses its spherical shape and luster, typical of pure melt surfaces. This is indicative of a significant change in the droplet composition, probably resulting from saturation with oxygen from the ZrO 2 ceramic plate. In addition, note that the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti droplet does not wet the ZrO 2 surface in this experiment, although the contact angle for this melt composition is approximately 60°, as reported in [ 6 ].

Thus, the effect of simultaneous contact with active and inert metals on the wetting of ZrO 2 is mutual and can be very intense, particularly before the metals melt, i.e., when the materials are in solid–solid contact.

Figure 3 shows a microphotograph of the contact area between the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti alloy and the ZrO 2 ceramic plate. The holding time after copper melting was 10 min. It is noteworthy that the transition layer at the interface was much thinner and more uniform in the study [ 7 ], which also focused on the contact interaction of copper–gallium–titanium melts with zirconium dioxide.

Figure 4 presents an experiment in which two samples were examined simultaneously. In one sample, a Cu–17.5 Ga foil was placed on a molybdenum plate, with a ZrO 2 ceramic plate positioned on top, and then a weighed portion of the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti alloy was placed on top of the ceramic plate. In vacuum heating and subsequent melting of the Cu–17.5 Ga and (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti alloys, the solid oxide was in contact with both inert and active melts. In the other sample, a (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti droplet was placed on the ZrO 2 surface without contact with the inert melt.

The results show that the active melt does not wet the ceramic sample and the droplet loses its spherical shape and luster when both melts—inert and active (sample on the left)—come into contact with the ceramic sample. If only the active melt is in contact with ZrO 2 (droplet on the right), the metal wets the ceramic sample, and the droplet retains its luster and spherical shape. Thus, the simultaneous interaction with inert and active melts does influence the contact processes, including those involving the active melt.

In the next experiment, the sample was arranged as follows: a weighed portion of copper placed beneath a ZrO 2 ceramic plate did not contact its surface until the copper melted (1356 K). A preliminary holding period at 1323 K was introduced to allow the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti melt droplet on the upper side of the plate to spread and reach an equilibrium contact angle. The weight of both droplets before the experiment was about 1.5 g (1.54 g for (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti and 1.48 g for copper). The results are shown in Fig. 5 .

After holding at 1323 K, the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti droplet spread over the ZrO 2 ceramic surface, reaching an equilibrium contact angle of 63° (Fig. 5 b ). After the copper melted and touched the lower side of the ZrO 2 plate, the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti droplet deviated from its spherical shape and a new phase formed near the contact area within the first 5 min of contact (Fig. 5 c ). With further holding, the droplet took an irregular shape and lost its typical metallic luster (Fig. 5 d , e ). It is also noteworthy that the copper droplet spread somewhat over the ZrO 2 ceramic surface, likely due to the dissolution of zirconium from nonstoichiometric ZrO 2 in the inert metal melt, as explained in [ 2 ]. However, as in [ 2 ], no wetting was achieved, probably because the number of interacting components also influences the processes in the systems studied.

Figure 6 shows a microphotograph of the contact area between the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti melt and the ZrO 2 ceramic plate for the sample presented in Fig. 5 . The holding time after the copper melt contacted with the ZrO 2 ceramic plate was 13 min. The interfacial surface exhibits a thick and apparently loose transition layer. This layer is much thicker than that observed in [ 7 ] but, unlike in Fig. 3 , its thickness is uniform. Thus, holding at 1323 K influenced the structure of the contact area.

In the next experiment, the weight of the Cu–17.5 Ga alloy was doubled (to 2.7 g), and the concentration of titanium added to this alloy was increased to 30 at.%. Holding at 1323 K was also used to form a (Cu–17.5 Ga)–30 Ti droplet and reach an equilibrium contact angle with the ZrO 2 ceramic surface. The results are shown in Fig. 7 . As seen, the simultaneous interaction of the active and inert melts with the substrate causes the active melt droplet to lose its luster and deform, as in the previous experiments. However, in this case, the droplet deforms over a longer period than in the previous experiment (Fig. 4 ). Hence, the amount of active melt and the concentration of titanium (or, more precisely, the ratio between the active and inert components in the system) influence the interaction process. Note also that copper spread until it reached wetting (θ < 90°) in this experiment, which was not observed in previous experiments; i.e., a greater amount of titanium in the active alloy intensifies interaction between the inert melt and ZrO 2 .

Figure 8 shows a microphotograph of the contact area between the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–30 Ti melt and the ZrO 2 ceramic plate for the sample presented in Fig. 7 . The holding time after the copper melt contacted with the ZrO 2 ceramic plate was 20 min. A transition layer is also present at the interface, but it contains much less of the dark, oxide phase compared to the samples in Figs. 3 and 6 . The increased amount of titanium obviously contributes to the deoxidation of the active melt, which in turn improves the wetting of the plate with the inert melt.

Figure 9 shows a microphotograph of the contact area between the active melt and the ZrO 2 ceramic plate for the sample studied in [ 2 ] using the ‘sandwich’ method. In this method, a copper foil was placed on a titanium bar, a ZrO 2 ceramic plate was positioned on top, and a weighed portion of Cu–17.5 Ga was placed above and then the assembly was heated in a vacuum. Thus, the copper–titanium melt formed beneath the ceramic plate, which further deoxidized through the dissolution of the titanium bar. Hence, the amount of titanium interacting with ZrO 2 was substantial. It is noticeable that, despite the heterogeneity of the solidified active metal, no transition layer is formed on the interface. This indicates that the deoxidation effect does take place.

Figure 10 presents an experiment in which a droplet of the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti active metal melt is located beneath a ZrO 2 ceramic plate, with the inert metal (copper) positioned on top. To conduct the experiment, a weighed portion of (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti was first melted in a vacuum on the ZrO 2 ceramic surface and held until a symmetrical droplet was formed and an equilibrium contact angle was reached, as in the experiments using the sessile drop method. After cooling, the sample was placed on a support with the solidified droplet facing downward. A weighed portion of copper was then placed on top of the plate, and the assembly was heated in a vacuum. The weight of both drops was about 1 g. In this experiment, smaller droplets were studied to prevent the active metal melt from coming into contact with the Al 2 O 3 ceramic support.

As in previous experiments, the active metal droplet loses its luster and regular spherical shape, while the inert metal droplet spreads slightly. However, in this case, there was noticeable ‘sagging’ of the lower (active) droplet, with a fillet formed near its base. This may be indicative of heterogeneous droplet composition and possibly even melt stratification. Noteworthy is also the visible expansion of the droplet, probably resulting from the dissolution of the ZrO 2 ceramic components in it.

Figure 11 shows a microphotograph of the transition area between copper and ZrO 2 in the sample presented in Fig. 10 . Some dissolution of the substrate in the metal is observed, which appears to improve the wetting of ZrO 2 by copper, though to a much lesser extent than reported in [ 2 ]. This again confirms that the amount of titanium in the active melt influences the interaction between ZrO 2 and the inert melt in the systems studied.

In addition to the copper–gallium–titanium system, copper–titanium alloys were studied, particularly Cu–10 Ti. For this system, the liquidus temperature is approximately 1273 K [ 8 ]. Therefore, a holding period at 1323 K was introduced to form a contact between the active melt and the ceramic sample, allowing the equilibrium contact angle to be reached. The weight of the active and inert melt droplets was close to 1.5 g. The results are shown in Fig. 12 .

The results are similar to the previous experimental findings: the droplet of the active metal loses its spherical shape and then its typical luster. The droplet of the active melt spreads slightly over the ceramic surface. However, some differences should be noted. The process is slower than that observed with the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti droplets, despite the lower titanium concentration. This may be due to the higher activity of titanium in the copper melt compared to the copper–gallium melts [ 9 ]. The changes on the droplet surface also occur ‘from the bottom up’: i.e., from the contact between the active melt and the ceramic sample to the top of the droplet. Moreover, the surface of the droplet becomes more prominent after an extended holding period.

Figure 13 presents an experiment in which a droplet of the Cu–20 Ti alloy, preformed on the ZrO 2 surface by vacuum melting, is positioned beneath the ceramic plate, while a copper droplet is placed on top.

The droplet deformed directly during the melting of copper, as in the experiment shown in Fig. 10 , and a fillet appeared at the droplet base, being indicative of heterogeneous droplet composition. After 1.5 min of holding (Fig. 13 d ), the droplet touched the graphite support and spread over it as a result of ‘sagging’. Despite the droplet’s irregular shape and loss of surface luster, the active alloy was in a liquid state. With further holding, the droplet’s surface lost its smoothness (Fig. 13 e ), and deformation (bending) of the ZrO 2 ceramic plate was observed. This may indicate that the active metal droplet crystallized in the interaction, inducing mechanical stresses that bent the ceramic plate.

In [ 2 ], germanium and Cu–20 Ge melts interacted most intensively with nonstoichiometric ZrO 2 . Figure 14 presents experiments in which germanium ( T melt = 1210 K) and (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti melts simultaneously interacted with the ZrO 2 ceramic sample. The ceramic plate was placed on an Al 2 O 3 ceramic support, with a weighed flat portion of germanium beneath and the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti alloy on top. In the melting process, the germanium droplet touched the lower surface of the ceramic plate, allowing the simultaneous interaction of two melts with the oxide to be observed.

In the case of germanium and (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti melts, the simultaneous interaction with ZrO 2 is very intense; the germanium droplet shows high adhesion to the ceramic surface and demonstrates wetting after 40 sec of contact (Fig. 14 b ). It should be noted that a fillet forms first at the droplet base (Fig. 14 b ), which is indicative of uneven melt composition. The droplet then spreads and forms a more symmetrical, probably spherical, surface (Fig. 14 c – e ). The active metal droplet, in turn, quickly deforms and loses its metallic luster (Fig. 14 b , c ) and, after prolonged holding, loses surface smoothness.

Figure 15 presents an experiment in which the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti and Cu–20 Ge melts (with the active melt droplet placed on top of the plate) simultaneously interacted with ZrO 2 .

After contact of the Cu–20 Ge melt with the ZrO 2 ceramic sample, as in the previous experiments, the active melt lost its regular spherical shape (Fig. 15 c , d ). However, it should be noted that, unlike in other systems, the Cu–20 Ge droplet did not spread over the ceramic surface. Moreover, after 10 min holding, the Cu–20 Ge melt separated from the lower side of the ceramic plate (Fig. 15 d ).

Figure 16 presents an experiment in which the Cu–20 Ge and (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti melts also interacted with ZrO 2 , but the Cu–20 Ge droplet was located on top this time. The contact between the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti droplet and ZrO 2 was formed by preliminary melting of this alloy on a ZrO 2 plate in a vacuum.

The (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti droplet loses its luster (Fig. 16 b ) and spherical shape (Fig. 16 c – f ), but the Cu–20 Ge droplet does not spread either. In 10 min after the Cu–20 Ge melting, the experiment’s temperature was increased to 1150°C, but this did not improve the ZrO 2 wetting with the Cu–20 Ge melt (Fig. 16 c ), although the droplet began to tremble at this temperature (Fig. 16 d , e ). The volume of the Cu–20 Ge droplet decreased, apparently because of evaporation, while the volume of the (Cu–17.5 Ga)–15 Ti droplet somewhat increased (Fig. 16 f ).

After the experiment, the solidified Cu–20 Ge droplet separated from the ceramic surface under its own weight. Thus, although the copper–germanium melt interacts with nonstoichiometric zirconia, this does not enhance adhesion. The oxygen deficiency in ZrO 2– x may be insufficient to ensure that the oxide is wetted by this inert melt. In [ 2 ], where the maximum possible oxygen deficiency in ZrO 2– x was reached, the copper–germanium melts showed the highest adhesion.

An experiment was conducted using the Cu–20 Ge alloy (inert melt) with a much larger amount of titanium in the active melt: Cu–50 Ti and Cu–20 Ge melts simultaneously contacted with ZrO 2 . The weight of the copper–titanium alloy was about 3 g and the weight of the copper–germanium alloy was about 1 g. The results are shown in Fig. 17 .

The interaction was quite intense. The Cu–50 Ti droplet noticeably lost its metallic luster in 1.5 min (Fig. 17 c ) and deviated from its spherical shape in 2 min (Fig. 17 d ). This behavior is characteristic of melt stratification (Fig. 17 d ), where two phases are present in the liquid, and one of them wets the solid substrate less effectively. Then (Fig. 17 e , f ) the ratio of the phases shifted in favor of the one that wetted the substrate less effectively. Despite the loss of luster, the droplet’s surface remained smooth and the droplet’s shape was relatively symmetrical, although not spherical. After 10 min holding (Fig. 17 f ), the droplet became uneven. It is also noticeable that the wetting of the substrate by the inert melt improves, contrastingly to the experiments with a lower amount of Ti (Figs. 15 and 16 ). Hence, the amount of Ti in these systems influences the contact interaction between the Cu–Ge alloy and ZrO 2 . In addition, it is noteworthy that the active melt spread across the ceramic plate, even to the extent of flowing over the edge of the plate (Fig. 15 f ).

The low adhesion of copper–germanium alloys in the experiments presented in Figs. 15 and 16 may be explained as follows: in the simultaneous interaction of active and inert melts with ZrO 2 , the active melt absorbs oxygen from the substrate, creating an oxygen deficiency and rendering the oxide nonstoichiometric (ZrO 2– x ). This allows the inert melt to dissolve zirconium from the substrate. As a result, the oxygen deficiency decreases and the ZrO 2 stoichiometry is restored. Germanium, particularly when alloyed with copper, evidently has a very high affinity for zirconium. In the case of the copper–germanium melt (as the inert melt) and a low amount of titanium in the active melt, the absorption of oxygen from ZrO 2 by the active melt is slower than the absorption of zirconium from the substrate by the copper–germanium melt. Zirconium dioxide shows a low oxygen deficiency directly beneath the copper–germanium droplet, indicating that its composition is close to stoichiometric. According to [ 2 ], only zirconium dioxide with high nonstoichiometry is wetted by metal melts in a vacuum. Therefore, the Cu–20 Ge melt did not wet the ZrO 2 ceramic sample as the active melt absorbed oxygen too slowly because of the low amount of titanium.

Conclusions

In the simultaneous interaction of active and inert metal melts with zirconium dioxide, the processes at the interfaces demonstrate mutual influence. On the one hand, the absorption of oxygen from ZrO 2 by the active melt promotes the dissolution of zirconium in the inert melt. As a result, the melt becomes activated and its adhesion to the substrate enhances. On the other hand, the dissolution of zirconia in the inert melt decreases the oxygen deficiency in ZrO 2 . Hence, more oxygen dissolves in the active melt, leading to oxygen saturation of the droplets. This results in effects such as the loss of metallic luster and spherical shape by the active melt droplets, stratification of the active melt, and an increase in the thickness of transition layers at the interfaces between the active melt and ZrO 2 .

These findings can be useful in the development of techniques for producing parts containing a solid oxide electrolyte layer between the metal materials, particularly in electrochemical and electronic devices.
