Abstract

This study proposes a heterogeneous recommendation model that does not rely on data sharing. Previous studies have predominantly focused on nested homogeneous domains that share data. However, this approach encounters issues as it could lead to diminished recommendation performance when there is a scarcity of redundant data within these domains. To overcome these challenges, we propose the HeteLFX model, which extracts and bridges the latent features (LF) of each domain. This model resolves the problems by leveraging the metainformation of domain items to generate an LF. LF is extracted for each domain, and bridges are established based on the relevance of the latent knowledge, thereby enabling heterogeneous recommendations. The efficacy of the HeteLFX model was assessed by comparing it with four other heterogeneous recommendation systems, which are variants of X-Map and NX-Map. The results revealed that the HeteLFX model improved performance by reducing the mean absolute error (MAE) by approximately 0.3, thereby underscoring the superiority of the model. Additionally, HeteLFX reduced the MAE by up to approximately 0.45, depending on the relevance of the data within the domain.

1. Introduction

Recommendation systems provide suggestions that align with individual preferences based on past behaviors ( Ko et al., 2022 , Wang et al., 2019 , Srifi et al., 2020 ). Traditional recommendation systems have made significant strides in enhancing user experience by mitigating data sparsity and the cold-start problem through methods such as matrix factorization and transfer learning . However, these systems often falter when recommending across distinct domains, where the challenge is not only the scarcity of shared user information but also the disparate feature spaces of items in different domains. Such limitations not only restrict the adaptability of recommendation systems but also hinder their potential to assist users in navigating different domains. Heterogeneous recommendations ( Lu et al., 2022 , Guerraoui et al., 2017 ) leverage associations between two domains to afford users the opportunity to explore new territories. This strategy aims to facilitate recommendations between domains, such as suggesting books in a reading app based on a userâ€™s interactions in a movie app from a different company, a scenario where traditional recommendation methods might struggle due to the lack of overlapping user information and the distinct nature of item features in each domain.
Fig. 1 . illustrates the disparity between existing heterogeneous recommendation research and our work. As shown in Fig. 1 (a), previous research ( Guerraoui et al., 2017 ) attempted to bridge this gap using overlapping users. However, this approach falls short when no overlapping users exist between different applications. Moreover, direct data sharing between two domains from distinct companies not only poses potential security risks but also involves issues pertaining to the privacy of personal information. Unregulated data sharing may inadvertently expose sensitive user information, thereby amplifying security and privacy concerns.
To overcome these challenges, we proposed HeteLFX, a heterogeneous recommendation system designed for domains that do not share data. HeteLFX utilizes latent knowledge embedded in the metainformation of each domain. For instance, movies carry information about different genres, whereas books bear knowledge about various fields and genres. Despite these two applications having no overlapping users or common items, they can identify associations by using latent genre knowledge. Our model exploits this latent knowledge to determine the associations between the domains. Fig. 1 (b) shows how our model indirectly forms connections by extracting latent features (LF) that contain latent knowledge from the metainformation of each domain. The proposed model aims to alleviate data security concerns and provide new possibilities for exploring heterogeneous recommendations.
In addition, the HeteLFX model contributes significant strategic implications for social media platforms and content providers. By adopting the HeteLFX model, these platforms can expand their content ecosystem, bridging the gap between different content areas. This approach not only attracts a broader audience by catering to diverse interests and preferences but also promotes user engagement and opens new avenues for content partnerships and collaboration. Moreover, by operating without direct data sharing across domains, the HeteLFX model addresses potential privacy and security concerns, making it particularly relevant for platforms aiming to enhance user engagement without compromising data security and user privacy. Thus, implementing HeteLFX can help platforms offer personalized and varied content recommendations while adhering to data protection standards, further driving the growth and diversity of the platform.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section 2 introduces related prior work. Section 3 presents the formulation of the heterogeneous recommendation problem. Section 4 describes the extraction of LF based on metainformation. In Sections 5 Heterogeneous recommendation (HeteLFX) , 6 Experiment , respectively, we present the new heterogeneous recommender system , HeteLFX, and discuss the proposed model based on experiments. Finally, Section 7 concludes the paper, summarizing the studyâ€™s findings and suggesting future work.

2. Related work

In this section, we review three important research areas relevant to our study: heterogeneous recommendations, cross-domain mediation, and privacy-preserving recommendations.

2.1. Heterogeneous recommendation

In the field of recommender systems , the role of heterogeneous recommendations is becoming increasingly important for improving diversity and accuracy. In this section, we introduce the existing research that has contributed to the advancement of heterogeneous recommendations.
The mixture of non-metric embeddings for recommendation (monomer) model ( He et al., 2017 ) expands the modeling of copurchase and cobrowsing relationships within the recommendation domain. The model provides a way to understand the complex and heterogeneous relationships between items in product recommendations and a means to consider the relevance of items based on their unique content. This paves the way for a deeper understanding of the interrelationships among items, which can improve the recommendation of heterogeneous items. However, the difficulty in capturing copurchase and cobrowsing relationships in different domains for different applications limits their application.
The X-Map and NX-MAP models ( Guerraoui et al., 2017 ) propose a heterogeneous recommendation method that leverages overlapping user profiles from two domains to create temporal profiles. These models assume homogeneous domains with overlapping data types and data from a two-domain perspective. However, these assumptions often do not hold in real-world scenarios, in which domain data often do not have overlapping users or exist in heterogeneous domains that encapsulate different types of users and categories of information ( Zhu et al., 2021 ). This inconsistency in assumptions poses challenges in generating reliable heterogeneous recommendations.
The proposed HeteLFX model addresses these issues by enabling heterogeneous recommendations to be mediated across different domains. This is achieved by indirectly linking the latent knowledge in each domain, assuming that it does not rely on data sharing. Because this process works under the assumption that it does not rely on direct data sharing, it can address the challenges faced in real-world applications.

2.2. Cross-domain mediation

Cross-domain mediation ( Berkovsky et al., 2007 ) is a field that integrates information and knowledge from different disciplines or domains. This area of research is particularly useful in recommender systems, in which integrating cross-domain knowledge can potentially increase the accuracy of personalized recommendations. Such an approach can address issues related to data sparsity and cold-start problems and contribute to the diversity of recommendations.
The graphical and attentional frameworks for dual-target cross-domain recommendation (GA-DTCDR) model ( Zhu et al., 2020 ) and partially overlapped cross-domain recommendation (POCDR) models ( Liu et al., 2022b ) both utilize cross-domain mediation mechanisms to enhance recommendation accuracy by embedding users and items, representing them in a lower-dimensional space for efficient computation. However, the degree of overlap between domains somewhat limits their application. If the domains do not overlap sufficiently, these models may struggle to find common features that would otherwise allow for accurate recommendations.
The cross-domain collaborative filtering model based on latent factor alignment (CDCFLFA) ( Yu et al., 2022 ) and Codebook-based knowledge transfer (CBT) ( Li et al., 2009 , Moreno et al., 2012 ) models demonstrates cross-domain mediation using the similarity of rating patterns. However, this method is limited by the assumption that there is a pattern association among homogeneous domains. This assumption poses a challenge when applied to heterogeneous domains, in which the rating patterns can be inherently different.
Models leveraging heterogeneous graph embeddings, such as the Heterogeneous and Clustering-enhanced Personalized Preference Transfer Model for Cross-Domain Recommendation (HCCDR) ( Xu et al., 2023 ) and Embedding Content and Heterogeneous Network for Cross-Domain Recommendation (ECHCDR) ( Li et al., 2020 ), utilize heterogeneous information networks (HIN) ( Liu et al., 2022a ) to account for the diverse semantics of relationships among various types of entities. These models construct networks with multiple types of nodes and links to consider the personal characteristics of users and the common features or attributes of items across domains, thereby facilitating cross-domain mediation. However, these approaches can lead to performance degradation in environments where common characteristics across heterogeneous domains are sparse.
Our study differs from these conventional models by focusing not on homogeneous domains but on the complex landscape of heterogeneous domains. In these scenarios, in which the domains are not uniform and exhibit distinct characteristics, previous models may struggle. We demonstrated cross-domain mediation in these heterogeneous domains by leveraging the associativity of latent knowledge within each domain.

2.3. Privacy-preserving recommendation

Growing concerns regarding the security of user data have made privacy protection an essential requirement for recommender systems ( Wang et al., 2018 ). This is particularly true for recommender systems that rely heavily on user data to provide accurate and personalized recommendations. Therefore, it is crucial to incorporate strategies to protect user privacy without compromising system performance and functionality.
A common approach to ensure privacy in recommender systems is to apply differential privacy (DP) ( Meng et al., 2018 , Wu et al., 2022 ). In response to the increasing privacy concerns, differential privacy adds a certain level of statistical noise to raw user data, obscuring personal information. This method enables recommender systems to generate personalized recommendations based on modified data without violating the privacy of individual users . In this manner, it encapsulates the inherent value of a recommender system: delivering personalized recommendations while safeguarding user data.
A prominent example of privacy preservation in action can be found in the recommendation sector that deals with cross-domains. The neural attentive transfer recommendation (NATR) model ( Gao et al., 2021 ) operates based on a unique privacy preservation principle. It shields user privacy by sharing item-specific information as opposed to user behavioral data. This approach was specifically designed to prevent the leakage of user privacy in cross-domain recommendation (CDR) settings, demonstrating a successful strategy for maintaining a delicate balance between delivering accurate recommendations and upholding user privacy.
In this study, we focus on a privacy-protecting latent feature extraction method that harnesses latent knowledge about users and items to preserve privacy while conducting heterogeneous recommendation tasks, particularly in a heterogeneous environment. Latent feature extraction enables the proposed HeteLFX model to develop a more nuanced understanding of user preferences without directly accessing personal data . Consequently, this contributes to the balance between user privacy and recommendation accuracy.

A typical problem setting for heterogeneous recommendations involves recommending items in a target domain D T using user data from a source domain D S . The source domain consists of users U S and items I S , whereas the target domain consists of users U T and items I T . 3.2. Heterogeneous recommendation without sharing domain data

Unlike the typical problem setting of heterogeneous recommendations, we do not directly share the users, items, and metainformation of D S and D T . Directly sharing data from the domains not only violates the security concerns of the data but also leads to sparsity, as each domain has information from other domains. Instead, we propose a solution that uses latent features to form a bridge, as illustrated in Fig. 1 .(b). Latent features contain the latent knowledge of each domain and can recommend items from other domain areas by learning their associations.

4. Latent features

In this section, we describe latent feature extraction, a method for extracting latent knowledge from a domain. The extraction process for the latent features consists of two steps. First, metainformation features are extracted based on the metainformation of items. Second, user and item features are generated based on the extracted metainformation features. Subsequently, these form the latent features through the interrelationship of user and item features.

5. Heterogeneous recommendation (HeteLFX)

To address the issue of heterogeneous recommendations without the need for data sharing, we harness latent features that capture the inherent knowledge of each domain. Fig. 2 shows an example of the overall architecture that leverages a bridge for latent features in each domain for heterogeneous recommendations in the movie and book domains.

Definition 5 Bridge

Given two domains, D S and D T , â€˜bridgeâ€™ is defined as establishing a relationship between two domains by considering the latent features of each domain.
The figure illustrates a heterogeneous recommendation system that extracts latent features from each domain to recommend a book (an item from another domain) to a movie user. This approach mitigates data security concerns by indirectly bridging domains rather than directly sharing data between them.
The proposed method exploits latent features to extract the intrinsic knowledge of each domain and employs a random forest algorithm to predict user ratings. This algorithm can predict associations between the latent features of the D S and D T by categorizing them based on impurity measures. The process is presented in Algorithm 2. To establish a connection between D S and D T , we extract latent features from all possible permutations of L U and L I in the D S . These features are then mapped using the most relevant combination determined by minimizing the mean absolute error (MAE) for each combination. Lastly, the model is used to compute the predictive scores for all items in the D T for a target user in the D S , and the top-N items with the highest predictive scores are recommended.

In this study, we evaluated the heterogeneous recommendation performance of the HeteLFX model. Four comparative models in the same environment were also assessed for performance comparison. We experimented with all the combinations using the four datasets summarized in Table 1 .

6.2.1. Performance comparison

In this section, we investigate the performance of the proposed model in comparison with other heterogeneous recommender systems, with the aim of evaluating their adaptability to heterogeneous recommendations. Our evaluation focuses on experimental analyses in cold-start scenarios and conditions of data sparsity, particularly examining the impact of overlapping users and the influence of user profile sizes. Additionally, we have broadened our analysis to include domains with varying degrees of content similarityâ€”namely, high content similarity (such as books and movies) and low content similarity (such as food and games). This expansion allows us to assess the impact of Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and explore the nuances of content similarity.
Impact of overlapping users.
The experiments presented in this section focused on the proportion of overlapping users, which is a significant factor in heterogeneous recommender systems. Overlapping users are those included in both the source and target domains, and as such users become less prevalent, they represent a cold-start situation with limited data. In our experimental evaluation, we set the ratio of overlapping users in the training dataset to be between 0.2 and 0.8. By manipulating this ratio to simulate different levels of cold-start situations, we compared the adaptability of the different models to these conditions. This experiment provided insights into the performance of different models under cold-start conditions in the context of heterogeneous recommendations.
The experimental results are shown in Fig. 4 . The experiment reveals that, in contexts of both high and low content relevance, the performance of all the heterogeneous recommendation models improved as the number of overlapping users increased. Furthermore, the proposed model outperformed the other models even when the number of overlapping users was sparse.
Impact of user profile.
This experiment concentrates on the impact of user profile size in a heterogeneous recommender system. The size of a user profile refers to the number of ratings a user has provided for items within the source and target domains. A reduction in the userâ€™s profile size signals a data sparsity situation. In our experimental evaluation, we established the ratio of user profile sizes in the training dataset to range between 1 and 5. By manipulating this ratio, we could simulate varying levels of data sparsity and compare the adaptability of different models under these conditions. This experiment yielded insights into the performance of various models under conditions of data scarcity within the context of heterogeneous recommendations.
The experimental results are shown in Fig. 5 . The findings demonstrate that, in contexts of both high and low content relevance, the performance of all the heterogeneous recommendation models improved as the user profile size increased. Notably, â€™the proposed model outperformed the other models even in situations where the user profile size was small, indicating data sparsity.

6.2.2. Impact of data associations across domains

In this subsection, we provide a detailed analysis of the performance results of the proposed model along with several other heterogeneous recommendation models across four domains: books, movies, games, and food.
The experimental results depicted in Fig. 6 demonstrate that our method outperforms the other methods across all combinations within these four domains. Additionally, all heterogeneous recommendation models demonstrated their highest performance in the heterogeneous recommendation experiments specifically targeting the movie and book domains, which were the most relevant.
The experimental results of the proposed model reveal that the book domain performs well across most domains, indicating its richness in latent knowledge that can be leveraged. For instance, this domain may encompass books related to food, games, and movies. In the heterogeneous recommendation experiment in the book domain, the movie and game domains proved to be the most effective, possibly because of their diverse genres. Moreover, in the movie domain, the book domains connected to the storyline showed the best performance. In the game domain, food-related domains are linked to satisfactory performance. Finally, in the food domain, book-related domains showcasing a wide range of knowledge yield the best results.
Our analysis underscores the significance of domain relevance in the performance of heterogeneous recommendations. Higher relevance of a domain is associated with improved performance, as it provides richer and more meaningful information for making recommendations. This underscores the importance of selecting the most suitable domain to achieve optimum performance in the context of heterogeneous recommendations.

6.3. Discussion

In this subsection, we discuss the proposed and comparison models based on the results of the experimental evaluation.
As shown in Fig. 4 , the proposed model exhibit improvements in cold-start scenarios. Specifically, it reduces the MAE by up to approximately 0.2 and 01 over the item-based comparison models X-Map-IB and NX-Map-IB and the user-based comparison models X-Map-UB and NX-Map-UB, respectively. This improvement can be attributed to our focus on addressing the challenges inherent in cold-start scenarios, in which there is no data sharing.
Furthermore, the sparsity results in Fig. 5 demonstrate that the proposed model outperforms item-based X-Map-IB and NX-Map-IB and user-based X-Map-UB and NX-Map-UB with MAEs of up to approximately 0.3 and 0.1, respectively. This accomplishment is significant because it shows how the proposed model can perform effectively despite the sparse data that often plague heterogeneous recommendation systems.

Instead, it employs a strategy to extract latent features that leverage latent domain knowledge to form bridges. This technique has the potential to improve performance even in situations where data sharing between domains is minimal or nonexistent. As opposed to other modelsâ€™ use of homogeneous domains that can be nested, the HeteLFX model leverages the latent knowledge of each domain.

7. Conclusion and future work

In this study, we proposed a novel heterogeneous recommendation system, HeteLFX, to address the problem of heterogeneous recommendations in which data are not shared. The proposed method utilizes the metainformation of items to extract potential features and constructs bridges as associations of potential knowledge in the source and target domains. This bridge enables the exchange and integration of potentially valuable knowledge between the source and target domains, without compromising user privacy. By leveraging such a bridge, our approach underscores the privacy-preserving prowess of HeteLFX, demonstrating how latent feature extraction can occur in the absence of direct data sharing across domains.
To evaluate the performance of the proposed model, we compared it with those of four other heterogeneous recommendation system models that share user data. The experiments were tailored to a heterogeneous recommendation environment, in which we intentionally hid the profiles of users in the target domain and instead used the profiles of users in the source domain to predict the ratings of items in the target domain. We conducted experiments focusing on two important aspects of heterogeneous recommendation: cold start and sparsity. These two factors form the basis for experiments that assess the adaptability of heterogeneous recommendation systems. In our experiments, the proposed HeteLFX model outperformed the comparison model, showing an improvement in the mean absolute error (MAE) of approximately 0.3, owing to the sparsity of duplicate users in both domains.
To verify the domain relevance and universality of HeteLFX, we validated it in four domains: book, movie, food, and game. These domains, each with its own characteristics and patterns, represent diverse contexts to which our model can adapt. Our results show that the performance of the model improves as the relevance of the domain increases, with HeteLFX reducing the MAE by up to approximately 0.45, depending on the relevance of the data within the domain.
Finally, we present future research directions for improving the performance of heterogeneous recommendation systems.
  • â€¢
    Heterogeneous recommendation based on a heterogeneous information network (HIN): Incorporating auxiliary information within a heterogeneous information network can increase the interpretability and performance of the model. Further utilizing HIN ( Liu et al., 2022a ) to extract latent features could uncover deeper domain associations, potentially heightening the effectiveness of heterogeneous recommendations.
  • â€¢
    Heterogeneous recommendation considering temporal dynamics: The current version of HeteLFX does not address changes in the temporal dynamics within each domain. We foresee that incorporating temporal dynamics, including a focus on seasonal sensitivity, into the model could allow us to capture evolving user preferences more accurately. Recognizing the significant impact of seasonal changes, especially in dynamic domains such as movies, books, and games, this addition aims to ensure that our recommendations align more closely with the changing needs and interests of users throughout the year, making our system more adaptable and responsive to temporal trends.
  • â€¢
    Incorporation of External Factors into HeteLFX: HeteLFX does not leverage external factors. In future research, we plan to explore the integration of various external factors, such as geographic-specific movie sentiments, cultural and traditional events based on user locations, and social interactions , into our recommendation model. This enhancement aims to address the challenge of sparse user data and capitalize on users who are actively up to date with trends. By enriching our model with these external dimensions, we expect to improve the performance of our recommendation system, making it more reflective of real-world user behavior and preferences.
